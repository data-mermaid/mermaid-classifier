The most recent experiment (Exp 7, label_smoothing=0.1) was REVERTED at balanced_accuracy=0.766, worse than the kept baseline of 0.785. Three specific telemetry signals explain why label smoothing failed and where the real opportunity lies:

1. **Label smoothing slowed convergence and degraded discriminative quality.** Exp 7's per-epoch curves show training_loss=7.886→6.729 (still 6.73 at final epoch 29) and val_loss=2.44→min 2.039 at step 25. Crucially, ref_accuracy stayed at 0.128 and val_accuracy at 0.126 throughout all 29 epochs — these can't both be raw classification accuracy given balanced_accuracy=0.766 at the end. The label_smoothing target distribution evidently broke the inline acc metric, but more importantly the model needed 29 epochs (vs Exp 2's 15) to plateau, and the final precision_macro jumped to 0.83 while recall_macro dropped to 0.766 (vs 0.79/0.785 in Exp 2). Label smoothing pushed the model toward more conservative, less-confident predictions that boosted precision on common classes but hurt recall on rare ones — exactly the wrong tradeoff for balanced_accuracy.

2. **The "overfitting" diagnosis behind label smoothing was based on a misread of the train/val loss gap.** Exp 2's ref_accuracy=0.833 vs val_accuracy=0.831 (gap 0.002!) shows the model generalizes nearly perfectly at the prediction level. The 0.44 loss gap (training 0.184 vs val 0.620) is artifact of dropout being active in train mode plus harder examples in val getting higher per-sample loss when confidently wrong — not memorization. Adding another regularizer on top of dropout=0.3 was therefore over-regularization, which is exactly what hurt rare-class recall.

3. **The current alpha=1e-4 L2 penalty is effectively zero.** In classifier.py, the L2 term is scaled by `(0.5 * alpha / mb_size)`: with alpha=1e-4 and batch_size≈200, the per-step penalty is ~2.5e-7 × ||W||², orders of magnitude smaller than the data loss gradient. So while dropout=0.3 acts at the activation level, there is essentially no weight-norm regularization. This is a structural gap: the model's weight norms are free to grow without check, which can cause the over-confident mid-bin behavior visible in calibration (Exp 2 bins 3–5 show gaps +0.15, +0.17, +0.16 — predictions hit 0.67–0.82 confidence with only 52–66% accuracy).

4. **Cross-branch error rate stayed high (0.731 in Exp 2, 0.746 in Exp 7).** Top confusions like Bare substrate→Macroalgae (13–14%, 284–306 samples), Soft coral→Hard coral (4–7%, 115–166 samples), and Other invertebrates→Macroalgae (8%, ~190 samples) involve large numbers of category-crossing errors. These are stable across runs and need better-conditioned weight matrices to disambiguate — proper weight decay tends to help here more than dropout, because it explicitly shrinks feature directions that overfit to spurious cues.

5. **Worst-source pattern persists.** coralnet:2055 (0.39 ba, 0.94 cross-branch), 2759 (0.44 ba, 0.83 cross-branch), 1776 (0.48 ba, 0.98 cross-branch) — these domain-shifted sources need better-regularized features, not more dropout. They suffer when the model has memorized source-specific shortcuts.

The diagnosis: dropout regularizes activations but not weight magnitudes, and the L2 penalty is effectively off. AdamW with proper decoupled weight decay (0.01) gives a principled, non-redundant regularizer that complements dropout without the recall-hurting bias that label smoothing introduced.
